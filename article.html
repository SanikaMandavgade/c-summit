The 2027 State of Guest Experience: What 250 Executives Told Us

Heather Bader
Chief Brand Officer, IHG Hotels & Resorts · 12 min read · 14 June 2026

Every January, we survey the C-level leaders attending the Summit before they arrive, asking where guest experience investment is actually going — not where strategy decks say it's going. This year's results, drawn from 250 respondents across 31 countries, surface a gap that should concern any board overseeing a hospitality portfolio.

Seventy-eight percent of executives told us guest experience is now a board-level priority. Yet only thirty-one percent could point to a single metric their organisation uses to measure it consistently across properties. The strategy has outpaced the instrumentation.

The measurement gap is the real story

Net Promoter Score remains the most commonly cited metric, used by sixty-six percent of respondents. But when we asked how NPS data actually changes operational decisions, the answers grew vague. Most groups collect the data. Few have built the feedback loop that turns a falling score into a specific, funded intervention.

"We have more guest experience data today than at any point in our company's history, and somehow less clarity about what to do with it."

That quote, from an anonymous COO respondent at a top-ten global hotel group, captures the tension running through this year's findings. Data collection has scaled. Decision-making infrastructure has not kept pace.

Where the budget is actually going

AI-driven personalisation tools captured the largest single share of new technology budget at thirty-four percent, followed by staff training and service design at twenty-eight percent. Notably, spend on integrating data across departments — the unglamorous plumbing work — ranked last, at just nine percent, despite being the change executives most frequently named as their top unmet need.

This mismatch between stated priority and actual spend recurred throughout the survey. It's the central theme we'll be unpacking across three breakout sessions at this year's Summit, and the starting point for the Boardroom Case for Guest Experience panel on the Mainstage.

What this means for 2027

The organisations pulling ahead share one trait: they have stopped treating guest experience as a service-quality initiative and started treating it as a data infrastructure problem with a service-quality outcome. That reframe changes who owns the budget, who reports to the board, and what gets funded first.
